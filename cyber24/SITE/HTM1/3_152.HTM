The sight of a monarch butterfly on the wing or sunning itself on the windowsill triggers reflexive reactions of delight and awe. The mystical power possessed by these delicate, beautiful insects -- which seem to appear from nowhere every spring and summer -- makes them welcome wherever they alight.

In fact, monarchs nest in the mountains west of Mexico City. Every year, after emerging from their cocoons and drying their wings for a moment or two, the newest generation starts fluttering north, eventually covering as much as 2,500 miles. This incredible journey illustrates the mysterious power of instinct, for it leads the butterflies on an unerring course to ancestral destinations they have never visited themselves -- monarch parent generations die before their offspring are born.

Since 1994, through a remarkable program sponsored by an interactive education provider called "Journey North," 25,000 middle-school students from Mexico to Canada have been using email and the Internet to track the migration each year. By reporting monarch sightings in their own hometowns, participants will help create a continentwide digital map of the migration in progress. And in weekly "ask the expert" sessions on the Net, they will be able to communicate directly with scientists and participate in current research.

From the monarch sanctuary in Mexico, an American exchange student emails updates to Minneapolis-based Journey North, sponsor of this continentwide, interactive science project. (Photograph by Peter Stone)

Every year instinct inexplicably leads the newest generation of monarchs to destinations they have never visited. On a chilly morning, students in Mexico blow warm breath on the still-dormant butterflies to animate them for their great journey north. (Photograph by Peter Stone)

Student participants located in the path of the butterfly migration will also study conservation; explore the geography, politics, and economics associated with environmental issues; and work with real-life applications of math and science associated with the monarch. In similar programs, students in other locales will concentrate on grizzlies in Yellowstone, caribou near Hudson Bay, humpback whales, peregrine falcons, and leatherback sea turtles, among other animals.

"We're using telecommunications in a way never before attempted in environmental education," says Elizabeth Donnelly, creator of the Minneapolis-based Journey North program. "These personal, human connections hold great promise for the future of conservation."
